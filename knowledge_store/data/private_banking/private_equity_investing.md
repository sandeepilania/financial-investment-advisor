Key Points:
- Private equity involves investing in non-public companies.
- These investments are typically illiquid and long term.
- Private equity may offer higher return potential with higher risk.
- Access is often limited to accredited or qualified investors.

Content:
Private equity refers to investments in companies that are not
publicly traded on stock exchanges. These investments are commonly
made through pooled funds or direct ownership structures.

Private equity strategies may include growth investing, buyouts,
turnarounds, or venture-style opportunities depending on the fund
mandate.

Because these investments are generally illiquid and may require
capital to remain committed for years, they are usually considered
appropriate only for investors with long time horizons and strong
liquidity planning.

In private banking, private equity is often discussed as part of
an alternatives allocation designed to enhance return potential
and diversify beyond traditional public markets.
